In this post I am reading the article ["Ground and Volume Decomposition as a Proxy for AGB from P-Band SAR Data"](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/10702464).

The paper is concerned with estimating Above-Ground Biomass (AGB) in forests, the mass of living organic material, excluding dead trees. This matters because living forests store carbon, whereas dead and decomposing organic matter releases it. Such estimates are crucial for global carbon accounting, climate policy, and financial mechanisms designed to protect the world's remaining forests.


To estimate AGB, researchers use radar measurements: a pulse of microwave energy is sent out, it hits the forest, and some fraction bounces back toward the antenna. The stronger the return signal, the higher the intensity.

Previous work found that radar backscatter intensity at exactly 30 meters above the ground correlates well with AGB. The explanation is that in dense tropical forests, the largest, oldest, and most massive trees tend to have their crowns concentrated around that height.

The ESA BIOMASS satellite mission aims to map global forest biomass using P-band SAR. SAR stands for Synthetic Aperture Radar; P-band refers to a specific range of microwave frequencies (roughly 300 MHz - 1 GHz, corresponding to wavelengths of 30 cm to 1 meter). These longer wavelengths are used because they penetrate deep into vegetation rather than bouncing off the treetops like shorter wavelengths do. BIOMASS is "an active sensor with all-weather day and night operating capabilities." The mission's full tomographic phase gives highly accurate 3D forest structure but is resource-intensive, so the question is whether a simpler approach during the faster interferometric phase could work nearly as well.

Radar waves can be transmitted and received in different orientations: horizontal (H) or vertical (V). A polarimetric system uses multiple combinations: HH (send horizontal, receive horizontal), VV, HV, VH. Polarimetry reveals what kind of surface is scattering, smooth like water or rough like a forest, but cannot produce the vertical distribution.

If two radar passes are made over a forest from slightly different positions, the two signals will be slightly out of phase. This is interferometry: by analyzing the phase difference, you can infer the vertical structure of the forest: canopy height, biomass distribution, and so on. Interferometry alone tells you where things are located vertically, but cannot distinguish ground from vegetation volume.

During the interferometric phase, BIOMASS collects both types of data. This allows a ground/volume decomposition: mathematically separating the total radar signal into what came from the ground and what came from the vegetation above. For a more detailed picture, tomography can be used, analogous to a medical CT scan. By making many passes from slightly different angles, you can reconstruct a 3D image of the forest interior, layer by layer.

Unfortunately, the ground/volume decomposition does not have a unique solution. To pin one solution down, one must make assumptions about the physical properties of the forest, for example that the ground is a perfect reflector, or that the vegetation has a certain density. The paper's conclusion is that ground/volume decomposition, even at its best, cannot match tomography. It can only crudely separate "ground" from "everything above ground," whereas only a signal isolated at a particular height predicts biomass well. What is more puzzling is that the best solution found by ground/volume decomposition comes from a counterintuitive, non-physical assumption, which raises concerns about the method's validity and its applicability to other forests, and calls for further research.
