Some notes on [AI as Normal Technology](https://knightcolumbia.org/content/ai-as-normal-technology) by Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor, which offers an alternative to the vision of AI as a potential superintelligence.

## Two competing worldviews

"Normal technology" and "impending superintelligence" are competing worldviews about the future of AI. This essay is a good counterweight to the more alarmist views you hear around.

The heart of the essay is that AI is a controllable tool, not an autonomous superintelligent species. Models are improving rapidly, but that does not necessarily mean society will be transformed at the same pace. New technology follows the path invention -> innovation -> adoption -> diffusion, and these stages happen at different timescales in different domains. In high-stakes domains where errors are costly, such as healthcare, criminal justice, and autonomous driving, deployed methods lag decades behind the research frontier. Diffusion is bounded by how fast people, organizations, and institutions adapt, not by how fast models improve. Software reaching billions instantly is availability, not adoption.

The essay brings a positive view of the future: as tasks get automated, a growing share of human labor becomes AI control and task specification. We can choose to use AI as a tool that helps us with specific tasks, rather than giving it autonomy over important decisions. The real danger of AI tools comes not from their capabilities but from the amount of power we give them.

One area explicitly not considered in the essay is the military use of AI. The authors argue that the dynamics and risks in the military domain are different from those in the general-purpose domain, due to the high cost of staying behind in military technology and the role of classified information, and should be considered separately.

## Nuclear power, not nuclear weapons

One interesting analogy is with nuclear weapons and nuclear power. AI could be put in the same category as nuclear weapons, but this analogy holds only if the harm and the cost of using that AI land on someone else. In that case, having a better AI gives you a strategic edge, and such situations genuinely produce arms races. Even if that scenario sounds real, it would be a concern only for military AI, which we have explicitly excluded from this essay. The authors claim that AI is more like nuclear power, where the cost of error is felt locally by the host of the AI. Deploying AI recklessly mainly produces local accidents, and governments and organizations generally have no incentive to hurt themselves. Moreover, such domains usually become highly regulated, potentially slowing down the development of the technology.

The authors claim that good regulation does not stop the progress and spread of technology, but instead attempts to reduce risks and uncertainty by funding research and tracking technology usage.

## Misuse prevention and real risks of AI

Another section of the essay talks about preventing misuse of AI models. The authors argue that defenses against misuse must be located elsewhere, not within the AI itself, since it lacks the context: attempting to make an AI model that cannot be misused is not a viable strategy, because the controls will be either too restrictive or ineffective. Instead, defenses should be implemented downstream, by improving critical infrastructure (such as better authentication and monitoring) and the social and legal infrastructure (such as better laws and regulations), and by creating multiple layers of protection. Moreover, AI should also be available to the defensive side, otherwise defenders will be at a disadvantage. For example, in cybersecurity, AI can be used to detect and respond to threats more effectively. Such defenses will help mitigate risk even from a misaligned AI that acts against the intent of the user.

One important point made in the essay is that the risks of AI come not necessarily from the technology itself, but from how it is used by people and institutions to advance their own interests, with AI "merely serving as an amplifier of existing instabilities in our society". So the real risks we should be concerned about are the spread of misinformation, manipulation, increased inequality, job losses, and concentration of power.

---

My takeaway is where we should point our worry: not at how advanced the models become, but at how much power we give them, and to whom.
